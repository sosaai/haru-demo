THE STORY

A boy from
Kanazawa who
learned to wait.

Hiro Tanaka grew up two streets from Omicho market, in Kanazawa, the city where his grandfather sold the morning catch from the Japan Sea. He learned to scale fish before he learned to read clocks.

At seventeen he moved to Tokyo and asked Master Saito for an apprenticeship. For three years he was only allowed to wash rice. For two more, only to make the vinegar. He didn't touch a piece of fish until his sixth year. He still believes those five years were the only ones that mattered.

板前は寿司を握る前に、米を知る。
An itamae must know rice before he forms his first nigiri.

— Master Takashi Saito, Tokyo · 1999
THE PATH

Twenty-six
years, six rooms.

From a back-of-house in Tsukiji to a counter of eight stools in Milano — a slow, deliberately quiet career.

1999 — 2004

Apprentice Sushi Saito · Tokyo

Five years under Master Takashi Saito. Washed rice for three. Made vinegar for two. Never touched a fish until the sixth year. Was permitted his first nigiri on his 22nd birthday.

2004 — 2009

Sous-itamae Sushi Mizutani · Ginza

Five years on the counter at the late Mizutani-san's three-star room. The discipline that still governs every plate at SHUN — rice at 36°C, fish dressed once, no second chances.

2009 — 2014

Senior chef Nobu · London Park Lane

Five years in the West. Met Aoi Sato (now SHUN's pastry chef) and his wife Carla. Began to think of opening his own room — small, no menu, only what he chose.

2014

Opens SHUN Via Solferino · Brera, Milano

Eight seats. Two seatings. Twelve to twenty courses. No à la carte for the first eighteen months. The room is full from week three.

2019

★ Michelin Star

SHUN becomes the second sushi room in Italy to be awarded a Michelin star. Retained at every guide since.

2026

Still at the counter Brera

Five nights a week, every service. Hiro has missed exactly four services in eleven years — three for the birth of his sons, one for his grandfather's funeral.

PHILOSOPHY

Rice first.
Fish second.
Hands last.

At SHUN the rice is older than the chef. The vinegar blend has been kept and re-fermented since 2014 — what you taste tonight has memory of the very first service. The fish is whichever piece arrived in the best window. The hands form one piece at a time, never two ahead.

We do not garnish. We do not plate. The piece is finished when it leaves the chef's fingers and the guest's hand picks it up. Three seconds between.

仕事 — Shigoto.
"The work." A piece is the work — not the plate around it.

— Hiro Tanaka
